E-Commerce Customer Lifecycle & Cohort Analysis (SQL)A comprehensive SQL-based analytical framework for evaluating customer retention, unit economics (ARPU), and revenue health using a business-driven cohort methodology.Phase 0 — Business FramingObjectiveThe primary objective of this project is to quantify customer loyalty and financial sustainability over time. By segmenting customers into acquisition cohorts, the analysis identifies which groups drive long-term revenue and where the business is experiencing "leakage" in the customer lifecycle.Analytical FocusRetention: Determining the "stickiness" of the product across different time-horizons.Monetization (ARPU): Tracking the evolution of average spend per user as a cohort ages.Churn: Identifying the "leakage points" in the customer lifecycle.Net Revenue Retention (NRR): Measuring the total financial health and growth/decay of specific customer groups.Business QuestionsIs customer retention improving or declining based on the month of acquisition?Do survivors of a cohort increase their spending over time (Expansion Revenue)?What is the impact of churn on the total revenue baseline per cohort?Phase 1 — Data Validation & CleaningObjectiveEnsure the transactional dataset is sanitized and structured for longitudinal analysis (tracking the same user over 12 months).Data Validation ChecksGranularity Control: Verified CustomerID presence; filtered out ~135k rows of anonymous traffic to ensure cohort tracking is possible.Negative Value Logic: Identified and isolated "C" prefix invoices (Returns) to ensure revenue is not artificially inflated.Structural Cleaning: Removed non-product transactions (e.g., POSTAGE, BANK CHARGES) to focus purely on inventory-driven revenue.Key Takeaway: Phase 1 establishes a "Clean Revenue" baseline, ensuring that retention and spending metrics are not skewed by administrative noise or incomplete data.Phase 2 — Retention Matrix ConstructionObjectiveMap the "Birth Month" of every customer and track their return behavior over an 11-month horizon.Key Analyses PerformedCohort Definition: Established the First_Purchase_Month for every unique identifier.Index Mapping: Calculated the month_index (the distance in months from the first purchase) using PERIOD_DIFF.The Retention Pivot: Constructed a matrix showing the percentage of users returning in each subsequent month.Key Takeaway: Phase 2 identified the Dec 2010 cohort as a statistical outlier with significantly higher long-term retention compared to mid-year acquisitions.Phase 3 — Monetization (ARPU) DynamicsObjectiveTransition from "counting people" to "counting value" by analyzing the Average Revenue Per User (ARPU).Analytical FocusDouble Aggregation: Sales were summed at the user level before being averaged at the cohort level to prevent transaction-frequency bias.Expansion Revenue Detection: Investigated if "surviving" customers spend more over time to offset the loss of churned users.Key Takeaway: While retention percentages naturally decay, the ARPU for the Dec 2010 cohort increased by ~78% between Month 0 and Month 11, indicating a high-value "loyal core."Phase 4 — Churn & Lifecycle AnalysisObjectiveDefine "The End" of the customer lifecycle and identify cohorts with high "Early-Death" rates.Key Analyses Performed90-Day Churn Definition: Using a MAX(InvoiceDate) logic, users were flagged as "Churned" if their last purchase exceeded 90 days from the dataset snapshot.Cohort Health Comparison: Directly compared "Active" vs. "Churned" counts per birth month.Key Takeaway: The April 2011 cohort was identified as a "Low-Quality" group, with churned customers outnumbering active ones (52% churn rate).Phase 5 — Synthesis & Net Revenue Retention (NRR)ObjectiveCombine Retention and ARPU into a single "Health KPI": Net Revenue Retention (NRR).Key OutputsNRR Percentage Matrix: Calculated as $(\text{Current Month Revenue} / \text{Starting Month Revenue}) \times 100$.Recovery Tracking: Quantified how much "lost" revenue from churn is recovered by the increased spending of survivors.The "Golden Goal" Metric: Identified months where NRR approaches or exceeds 100% (Negative Churn).Key Takeaway: The Dec 2010 cohort recovered 89.59% of its original revenue by Month 11, despite losing 30% of its customer base, proving the power of expansion revenue.Phase 6 — Validation, Assumptions & LimitationsValidation NotesImmature Data: Churn rates for late-2011 cohorts are "immature" due to the 90-day window and require a longer observation window for full confirmation.Wholesale Bias: High ARPU in the Dec 2010 cohort suggests a mix of B2B and B2C customers, which may skew "typical" consumer benchmarks.LimitationsTop-line Focus: Analysis focuses on Revenue; profitability and margins are not included in this dataset.Causality: Results are associational; specific marketing triggers for cohort behavior are not available.Phase 7 — Final Packaging & Project OverviewRepository Structuresql/01_cleaning.sql — View definitions and noise removal.sql/02_retention.sql — Pivot logic for the retention matrix.sql/03_monetization.sql — ARPU and NRR calculations.README.md — Business narrative and insights.Tech StackSQL: MySQL 8.0 (CTEs, Window Functions, Period Math).Concepts: Unit Economics, Cohort Analysis, Expansion Revenue, Lifecycle Marketing.Project SummaryThis project demonstrates an end-to-end customer lifecycle analysis, moving from raw transactional data to validated growth KPIs. It provides the framework necessary to distinguish between "High-Volume" acquisition months and "High-Quality" loyalty months.
